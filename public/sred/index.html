SR&ED Strategy

SR&ED is not a tax credit.
It is a capital structure decision.

Most consultants optimize individual claims. Execom structures companies so SR&ED eligibility compounds over time.

Canada's Scientific Research and Experimental Development program is one of the largest sources of non-dilutive capital available to technology companies. Yet most firms treat it as an annual tax exercise. Execom approaches SR&ED differently—as a structural component of corporate architecture.

The Industry Problem

Most SR&ED consultants are optimized for a single outcome: maximizing the size of the current year's claim.

That approach ignores the structural factors that determine whether a company will remain eligible in the first place.

Eligibility is shaped by decisions made long before the claim is written:

  • How R&D entities are structured
  • Where intellectual property sits
  • How technical work is documented
  • How capital is deployed across subsidiaries

When these foundations are wrong, even large claims eventually plateau—or disappear entirely.

The Execom Approach

Execom treats SR&ED as a capital architecture problem.

Instead of asking “How large can this year's claim be?”, the question becomes:

How should the company be structured so eligibility persists—and scales—as the business grows?

The advisory framework focuses on four layers.

Corporate structure

How R&D entities and operating companies interact.

Technical eligibility

How development work is organized and documented.

Financial architecture

How grants, SR&ED credits, and equity capital interact.

Long-term claim stability

Ensuring eligibility persists through product evolution, funding rounds, and international expansion.

SR&ED should compound like capital, not fluctuate like a tax deduction.

Fee Structure

The SR&ED consulting industry typically charges between 20% and 30% of recovered credits.

Execom charges 5%.

The reason is simple: the firm is not structured like a traditional claim-preparation consultancy.

Most SR&ED firms operate large claim-production teams and build their economics around high contingency fees. Execom operates as a strategic advisory practice. The goal is to help founders design systems where SR&ED eligibility becomes predictable and durable.

Lower fees follow naturally from that model.

When Companies Engage Execom

Companies typically engage Execom at one of three moments.

Early stage

A company beginning meaningful R&D wants to structure its technical work and corporate entities correctly from the start.

Growth stage

A scaling company wants to stabilize and expand SR&ED eligibility as engineering teams grow.

Post-plateau

A company whose SR&ED claims have stagnated wants to understand why eligibility has stopped compounding.

In each case the advisory question is the same: how should the organization be structured so SR&ED remains a reliable capital source?

SR&ED as Part of Capital Strategy

SR&ED is not isolated from the rest of a company's financing.

It interacts with:

  • Grant programs
  • Venture financing
  • IP ownership structures
  • International expansion
  • Product development cycles

When treated correctly, SR&ED can become a predictable layer of non-dilutive capital that supports long-term R&D investment.

When treated incorrectly, it becomes an inconsistent annual exercise with diminishing returns.
